Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is the most practical pattern for grounding LLM responses in your own data. LangChain gives you a composable toolkit to wire it up quickly.

## What the Demo Does

The sandbox below shows a complete pipeline:

1. **Ingest** — Load a text corpus and split into chunks
2. **Embed** — Convert chunks to vectors using OpenAI embeddings
3. **Store** — Index vectors in FAISS for fast similarity search
4. **Retrieve** — At query time, fetch the top-k most relevant chunks
5. **Generate** — Feed chunks + question to the LLM, get a grounded answer

Explore it live, fork it, and modify the documents or chain configuration.

## Key Concepts

### Document Loaders

LangChain provides loaders for nearly every data source — PDFs, web pages, Notion, databases, S3. They all produce a uniform `Document` object with `page_content` and `metadata`.

### Text Splitters

Large documents must be chunked before embedding. The `RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter` is the go-to — it splits on paragraphs → sentences → words, preserving semantic boundaries as much as possible.

**Chunk size trade-offs:**

| Chunk Size | Retrieval Precision | Context Coverage |
|------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| 256 tokens | High (narrow focus) | Low |
| 512 tokens | Balanced | Balanced |
| 1024 tokens | Lower (more noise) | High |

### FAISS vs. Managed Vector DBs

FAISS is perfect for prototyping and datasets under ~1M vectors. For production, consider Pinecone, Weaviate, or pgvector depending on your infra constraints.

### The RetrievalQA Chain

`RetrievalQA` wires retriever → LLM in two lines. Under the hood it's a prompt template that injects retrieved context before the question.

## When to Use RAG vs. Fine-tuning

**Use RAG when:**
- Your data changes frequently
- You need source citations
- Your dataset is too large for context windows
- You want to control what the model "knows" at runtime

**Fine-tune instead when:**
- You need to change *how* the model writes (style, format)
- Your task requires knowledge baked deeply into weights
- Latency is critical and you can't afford retrieval overhead
